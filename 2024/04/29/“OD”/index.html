OD

发表于2024-04-29|更新于2024-05-12
|阅读量:

Recently, I had a conversation with a doctor about the issue of drug abuse. Some of my friends in a certain online community have been seeking hallucinogenic experiences through excessive consumption of substances like dextromethorphan and methamphetamine hydrochloride, which they refer to as ‘OD’ (overdose).

The effects of drug abuse can vary, ranging from hallucinations and euphoria to dissociation, among other unpredictable outcomes. It’s like a loophole in the regulation of drugs within the country.

I was astonished to learn that the sleep aid medication I used, Sinose, is classified as a Class II controlled substance, on par with substances like morphine and heroin. It’s truly mind-boggling! While certain substances serve as legitimate medications when used appropriately, they can become drugs of abuse when misused.

Individuals are only allowed to purchase one box of this medication from a hospital, but some people go to multiple hospitals to buy numerous boxes. Take the case of that

The drugs used in OD, when purchased normally, are completely harmless. Take dextromethorphan, for example; it’s just a cough suppressant. If someone has a cough, one or two pills are sufficient.

But these individuals are swallowing a dozen or more pills, chasing after the side effects.

I recently learned that there are two hundred million addicts in India. Unlike South America, known for its export of drugs, India produces and consumes its own, so awareness is relatively low.

With a significant wealth gap, drug and substance abuse isn’t closely monitored for stability. If the poor die, so be it; there are always more people. Those with money buy heroin and cocaine, while the less fortunate resort to cough medicine or tranquilizers. For those with nothing, they turn to burning industrial glue, nail polish, or even resort to scorpion, centipede, or snake venom to achieve hallucinations by damaging their brain’s neural pathways.
